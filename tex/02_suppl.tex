\section{Theory}

\subsection{Preferential Prey Model}

We used the preferential prey model to generate initial food webs \cite{johnson_trophic_2014}.
The preferential prey model requires four parameters to build a food web: the number of (trophic) species $S$, the number of producer species $P$, the expected number of trophic links $N_l$, and trophic omnivory $\theta$ ($\theta > 0$).
In this model, a food web begins with $P$ primary producers, indexed as species $k = 1, 2, ..., P$.
Then, consumer species with index $k = P + 1, P + 2, ..., S$ are sequentially introduced and given their first prey $q$ randomly from those introduced earlier.
Consumer species $k$ choose, if any, additional prey $q'$ ($q' \ne q$) according to the trophic position of the first prey.

The number of additional prey, denoted as $\kappa_k$, is drawn randomly from a binomial distribution $\mbox{Bin}(a_k - 1, f_k)$, where $a_k$ is the number of species present upon the introduction of species $k$ and $f_k$ is the probability that existing species serving as prey for species $k$.
The parameter $f_k$ is assumed to be a realization of a Beta distribution:

\begin{equation}
    f_k \sim \mbox{Beta}\left(1, \frac{(S + P - 3)(S - P)}{2(N_l - S + P)} - 1\right)
\end{equation}

This parameterization ensures that the realized number of trophic links $n_l$ has the expected value $\mathbb{E}(n_l) = N_l$ over an ensemble of food webs (Lemma \ref{lemma-beta-tilde}).

The probability of choosing prey $q'$, denoted as $Q_{q'}$, is proportional to trophic distance between $q$ and $q'$:

\begin{equation}
    Q_{q'} \propto e^{-\theta^{-1} |\tau_{q'} - \tau_q|},
\end{equation}

where $\tau_q$ is the structural trophic position of species $q$.
The trophic position of producers is one, and
consumer trophic positions are given as the average of prey trophic positions plus one:

\begin{equation}
    \tau_k = 
    \begin{cases}
    1 & ~\text{for producers}~ (k=1, 2, \ldots, P),\\
    1 + |A_{k}|^{-1} \sum_{q \in A_{k}} \tau_q & ~\text{for consumers}~ (k = P+1, P+2, \ldots, S),
    \end{cases}
    % \label{eq:def_tp}
\end{equation}

where $A_{k}$ and $|A_{k}|$ denote the set and the number of consumable prey for species $k$, respectively.

\begin{lemma}
\label{lemma-beta-tilde}
    Let $f_k$ be a random variable that follows a Beta distribution as:

    \begin{equation}
        f_k \sim \mbox{Beta}(1, \tilde{\beta}),
    \end{equation}

    where

    \begin{equation}
        \tilde{\beta} = \frac{(S + P - 3)(S - P)}{2(N_l - S + P)} - 1.
        \label{eq:beta-tilde}
    \end{equation}

    Then, the realized number of trophic links $n_l$ has the expected value of $\mathbb{E}(n_l) = N_l$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    The realized number of trophic links $n_l$ is written as:

    \begin{align}
        \begin{split}
            n_l &= \sum_{k = P + 1}^{S}(1 + \kappa_k)\\ 
                &= (S - P) + \sum_{k = P + 1}^{S} \kappa_k.
        \end{split}
    \end{align}

    Recall that $\kappa_k \sim \mbox{Bin}(a_k - 1, f_k)$ and $f_k \sim \mbox{Beta}(1, \tilde{\beta})$.
    Then, statistical theory suggests that the variable $\kappa_k$ follows a Beta-binomial distribution as $\kappa_k \sim \mbox{Beta-binomial}(a_k - 1, 1, \tilde{\beta})$, whose expected value is $\mathbb{E}(\kappa_k) = (a_k - 1) / (1 + \tilde{\beta})$.
    Thus, we obtain

    \begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{E}(n_l) 
        &= (S - P) + \sum_{k = P + 1}^{S} \mathbb{E}(\kappa_k)\\
        &= (S - P) + \frac{1}{1 + \tilde{\beta}} \sum_{k = P + 1}^{S} (a_k - 1)\\
        &= (S - P) + \frac{1}{1 + \tilde{\beta}} \sum_{k = P + 1}^{S} (k - 2)~~~(\because a_k = k - 1)\\
        &= (S - P) + \frac{(S + P - 3)(S - P)}{2 (1 + \tilde{\beta})}.
    \end{split}
    \end{align}

    Using Equation \ref{eq:beta-tilde}, we yield

    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}(n_l)  = N_l.
    \end{equation}
    
\end{proof}

\subsubsection{Habitat-level occupancy dynamics}
Our model presented in the main text was derived from mechanistic assumptions operating at the level of individual habitats.
We first specify ecologically realistic processes governing establishment, dispersal, and extinction at this local scale, and then scale these processes to metapopulation occupancy dynamics using a spatial mean-field approximation \citep{ovaskainen_asymptotically_2006}.
Thus, the resulting metapopulation model is not formulated heuristically but emerges directly from the underlying habitat-level ecological dynamics, as described below.
Table \ref{tab:symbol-notation} lists key parameters in our model with their interpretations and specifications.

Let $p_{ik}$ ($0 \le p_{ik} \le 1$) be the probability of habitat $i$ being occupied by species $k$.
We write the temporal dynamics of $p_{ik}$ as

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d p_{ik}}{d t} = c_{ik} \phi_{ik}'(\eta_i - p_{ik}) - \mu_{ik} p_{ik}
    \label{eq:dm0}
\end{equation}

where $c_{ik}$ is the establishment probability, $\phi_{ik}'$ is the number of effective propagules, and $\mu_{ik}$ is the extinction rate.
The term $\eta_i \in [0,1]$ denotes the probability that habitat $i$ is suitable for species $k$.
For producers, $\eta_i=1$ for all habitats because all habitats are potentially colonizable.
For consumers, $\eta_i$ represents the probability that at least one prey species is available at habitat $i$.
In what follows, we use Equation~\ref{eq:dm0} as the baseline model describing the temporal evolution of occupancy probability at a single habitat.

\textit{Colonization} --
We define the establishment probability $c_{ik}$ based on resource availability for producers and prey availability for consumers:

\begin{equation}
    c_{ik} 
    =
        \begin{cases}
        r_0 + bu_i
        &\text{for producers}~(k \in 1, 2,\ldots,P),\\
        |A_k|^{-1} s_{ik}
        &\text{for consumers}~(k \in P + 1, P+2,\ldots,S).
        \end{cases}
    \label{eq:r-eq}
\end{equation}

For producers, $r_0$ is the minimum resource supply, and $u_i$ is the upstream river length at habitat $i$.
The coefficient $b$ controls the downstream accumulation rate of resource and is constrained as
$0 \le b \le L^{-1}(1-r_0)$ to ensure that $0 \le c_{ik}\le 1$.
Therefore, the model captures a downstream increase in primary production, a widespread pattern observed in river networks \citep{allan_stream_2007, finlay_stream_2011}.
For consumers, the term $s_{ik}$ denotes prey richness, defined as the expected number of prey species available at habitat $i$:
$s_{ik}=\sum_{q \in A_k}p_{iq}$,
where $q$ indexes prey species in the set $A_k$ (see Table~\ref{tab:symbol-notation}).
We normalize prey richness by the total number of potential prey species $|A_k|$ so that consumer establishment is proportional to relative prey availability.
We did not include predation effects on the establishment probability because newly colonizing prey populations are expected to occur at low densities, where predation pressure should be weak given that many consumers exhibit functional responses targeting more abundant prey \citep{dunn_predator_2020}.

We express the number of effective propagules $\phi_{ik}'$ as

\begin{equation}
    \phi_{ik}' 
    =
    g_{k} \sum_{j}^{N} \exp(-\delta_k d_{ij}) p_{jk},
    \label{eq:clnz-local}
\end{equation}

where $g_k$ is the number of propagules produced by species $k$, and $N$ is the total number of habitats in the ecosystem (recall that $N=hL$, where $h$ is habitat density per unit river length).
The dispersal kernel $\exp(-\delta_k d_{ij})$ describes the probability that propagules produced in source habitat $j$ reach target habitat $i$, separated by network distance $d_{ij}$.
Smaller $\delta_k$ values indicate weaker distance decay and therefore greater dispersal capability.
This formulation explicitly incorporates dispersal limitation imposed by the spatial configuration and connectivity of the river network \citep{hanski_metapopulation_1999}.

\textit{Extinction} --
We express the extinction rate $\mu_{ik}$ as a function of disturbance, prey scarcity, and predation:

\begin{equation}
    \mu_{ik} 
    = 
    \underbrace{\mu_{k}^{(0)} (1 + \rho_i u_i)}_{\text{Disturbance}} + 
    \underbrace{\mu_{k}^{(1)} \left(1 - \frac{s_{ik}}{|A_k|} \right)}_{\text{Prey scarcity}} + 
    \underbrace{\mu_{k}^{(2)} s_{ik}'}_{\text{Predation}}.
    \label{eq:extn-local}
\end{equation}

The disturbance component consists of local stochastic disturbance and downstream disturbance cascades.
The first component represents disturbance occurring independently at each habitat.
The second component represents additional extinction risk caused by downstream disturbance cascades, which increases with the synchrony probability $\rho_i$ and upstream river length $u_i$.
We assume this formulation because disturbances can propagate downstream as water flows \cite{massoudieh_biogeochemical_2010, nakamura_disturbance_2000, sarremejane_drought_2021, larsen_geography_2021, allan_stream_2007}.
Such cascades, however, are expected to attenuate with travel distance.
We therefore assume that the probability of synchronized disturbance at habitat $i$ declines exponentially with downstream travel distance $d_\rho$ from the disturbance origin:

\begin{equation}
    \rho_i = \exp(- \nu d_{\rho}),
\end{equation}

where $\nu$ defines the distance decay rate of the downstream disturbance cascade.
Smaller values of $\nu$ correspond to slower decay in synchrony and, consequently, a broader spatial extent of the cascade.

In the second term, we assume that extinction risk decreases with increasing prey richness at habitat $i$.
Specifically, $\mu_k^{(1)}$ denotes the extinction rate in the complete absence of consumable prey.
The extinction risk caused by prey scarcity is reduced by the factor $|A_k|^{-1}s_{ik}$, which represents the ratio of available prey richness to the total number of potential prey species (diet breadth).
We set $\mu_k^{(1)}=0$ for producers because they do not require prey for persistence.
This modeling approach differs from previous models, in which predators experience higher extinction rates than prey regardless of local patch conditions at the level of individual habitats (i.e., whether prey are present or absent) \citep[e.g.,][]{holt_food_2002, takimoto_effects_2012, calcagno_constraints_2011}.
Although such a simplified formulation may be appropriate for systems consisting of simple food chains, it is unsuitable for realistic food webs in which consumers can maintain populations through interactions with multiple prey species.

In the predation term, extinction risk is assumed to increase linearly with expected predator richness $s_{ik}'$ at habitat $i$, defined as the sum of occupancy probabilities for potential predators:
$s_{ik}'=\sum_{q \in A_k'}p_{iq}$,
where $q$ indexes predator species in the set $A_k'$ (see Table~\ref{tab:symbol-notation}).
The parameter $\mu_k^{(2)}$ determines the strength of the effect of predator richness on extinction risk.

\subsubsection{Mean-field approximation}
We describe the temporal dynamics of the fraction of habitats occupied (hereafter, ``occupancy'') by applying a spatial mean-field approximation to Equation~\ref{eq:dm0}, thereby dropping the habitat subscripts $i$ and $j$ \citep{ovaskainen_asymptotically_2006}.
As shown below, this approximation naturally expresses the establishment probability $c_k$, effective propagule supply $\phi_k$, and extinction rate $\mu_k$ as explicit functions of total river length $L$ and branching rate $\lambda_b$.
Importantly, no additional ecological assumptions are introduced at this stage; the metapopulation model follows directly from the mechanistic habitat-level processes specified above.

Let $p_k$ denote the fraction of habitats occupied by species $k$.
The occupancy dynamics are

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dp_k}{dt} = c_k \phi_k' (\eta_k - p_{k}) - \mu_{k} p_{k}.
\end{equation}

The term $\eta_k$ represents the fraction of suitable habitats as

\begin{align}
\eta_k =
    \begin{cases}
        1 &\text{for producers}~(k \in 1, 2,...,P),\\
        1 - \prod_{q \in A_k} (1 - p_q) &\text{for consumers}~(k \in P + 1, P + 2,...,S).
    \end{cases}
\end{align}

For producers, $\eta_k = 1$ because all habitats are suitable for colonization.
For consumers, $\eta_k$ represents the fraction of habitats containing at least one prey species.

The establishment probability becomes

\begin{align}
    c_k 
    &=
    \begin{cases}
        r_0 + b \hat{u} \qquad &\text{for producers},\\
        \hat{s}_k |A_k|^{-1} \qquad &\text{for consumers}.
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:estb-prob}
\end{align}

where $\hat{u} \equiv \mathbb{E}(u_i)$ is the expected upstream river length, and
$\hat{s}_k \equiv \mathbb{E}(s_{ik}) = \sum_{q \in A_k} p_q$ is the metapopulation-level expression of prey richness.
Under the spatial mean-field approximation, prey richness is represented by the summed occupancy of all potential prey species.

By Corollary~\ref{collorary-d}, the effective propagule supply $\phi_k'$ can be expressed as a function of the expected network distance $\hat{d} \equiv \mathbb{E}(d_{ij})$ as

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \phi_k'
        &=
        g_k\,N\,p_k\,\mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp(-\delta_k d_{ij})\bigr)\\
        &\approx
        g_k\,N\,p_k\,
        \underbrace{
        \left[
            1 - \delta_k\,\hat{d}\,\mathrm{erfcx}
            \left(
                \frac{\delta_k\,\hat{d}}{\sqrt{\pi}}
            \right) 
        \right]
        }_{\text{Fraction of effective propagules}},
        \qquad
        \lambda_b L \gg 1,
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where $\mathrm{erfcx}(\cdot)$ denotes the scaled complementary error function.
The product $g_k N$ represents the total propagule pool produced across the entire ecosystem when all habitats are occupied.

The extinction rate becomes

\begin{equation}
    \mu_{k} = 
        \mu_{k}^{(0)} (1 + \hat{\rho} \hat{u}) + 
        \mu_{k}^{(1)} \left(1 - \frac{\hat{s}_k}{|A_k|} \right) + 
        \mu_{k}^{(2)} \hat{s}_k',
    \label{eq:mean-extn}    
\end{equation}

where $\hat{s}_k' \equiv \mathbb{E}(s_{ik}' = \sum_{q \in A_k'} p_q)$ is the metapopulation-level expression of predator richness, obtained by summing the occupancies of potential predator species.
By Corollary~\ref{collorary-rho}, the expected synchrony probability $\hat{\rho}$ is a nonlinear function of network diameter $\hat{\mathcal{L}}$,

\begin{equation}
    \hat{\rho} \equiv \mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp({-\nu d_{\rho}})\bigr)
    \approx
    \frac{2}{\nu \hat{\mathcal{L}}} 
            - 
            \frac{\pi}{\nu^2 \hat{\mathcal{L}}^2} 
            \left[
                1 
                - 
                \mathrm{erfcx}
                \left(
                \frac{\nu \hat{\mathcal{L}}}{\sqrt{\pi}}
                \right)
            \right],
    \qquad
    \lambda_b L \gg 1.
\end{equation}

Finally, factoring $p_k$ out of $\phi_k'$ ($\phi_k' = \phi_k p_k$) yields the metapopulation model presented in the main text,

\begin{equation}
    \frac{dp_k}{dt} = c_k \phi_k p_k (\eta_k - p_{k}) - \mu_{k} p_{k}.
    \label{eq:master}
\end{equation}

In this model, $c_k$, $\phi_k$, and $\mu_k$ are functions of $\hat{d}$, $\hat{u}$, and/or $\hat{\mathcal{L}}$, which characterize the geometric structure of the river network.
These network properties scale as
$\hat{d}, \hat{u}, \hat{\mathcal{L}} \propto L^{\frac{1}{2}} \lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ in the regime $\lambda_b L \gg 1$ (Propositions \ref{proposition-mu-d}, \ref{proposition-u}, and \ref{proposition-diameter}).
Thus, our model explicitly links ecosystem structure to the colonization--extinction dynamics of constituent species.

\subsection{Laplace Transform}

\begin{corollary}
\label{collorary-d}
    Consider a population of random branching networks with total river length $L$ and branching rate $\lambda_b$.
    Let $d_{ij}$ denote the network distance between two random points $i$ and $j$ selected uniformly at random, and define
    
    \begin{equation}
    \hat{d} \equiv \mathbb{E}\bigl(d_{ij}\bigr). 
    \end{equation}

    Then, the Laplace transform of $d(i,j)$ can be approximated as

    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp (-\delta d_{ij}) \bigr)
        \approx 
        1 - \delta\,\hat{d}\,\mathrm{erfcx}
        \left(
        \frac{\delta\,\hat{d}}{\sqrt{\pi}}
        \right),
        \qquad 
        \lambda_b L \gg 1,
    \end{equation}

    where $\mathrm{erfcx}(\cdot)$ denotes the scaled complementary error function.
    It follows that the Laplace transform of $d_{ij}$ is an explicit function of $L$ and $\lambda_b$ given the asymptotic scaling $\hat{d} \propto L^{\frac{1}{2}} \lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ (Proposition \ref{proposition-mu-d}).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Under the assumption of random bifurcation, the distribution of $d_{ij}$ converges to a Rayleigh distribution \citep{ojeda_distance_2022}.
Therefore, the Laplace transform of $d_{ij}$ can be approximated by that of the corresponding Rayleigh distribution:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp (-\delta d_{ij}) \bigr)
    \approx
    1 - \delta\,\hat{d}\,\mathrm{erfcx}
    \left(
    \frac{\delta\,\hat{d}}{\sqrt{\pi}}
    \right),
    \qquad
    \lambda_b L \gg 1.
\end{equation}

It follows that the Laplace transform of $d_{ij}$ is an explicit function of $L$ and $\lambda_b$ given the asymptotic scaling $\hat{d} \propto L^{\frac{1}{2}} \lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ (Proposition \ref{proposition-mu-d}).

\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}
\label{collorary-rho}
   Consider a population of random branching networks with total river length $L$ and branching rate $\lambda_b$. 
   We assume that disturbance events occur uniformly over the network, and denote by $d_{\rho}$ the distance from a disturbance origin to a given habitat. 
   Under this setting, the Laplace transform of $d_{\rho}$ can be approximated as

    \begin{equation}
        \mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp({-\nu\,d_{\rho}})\bigr)
        \approx
        \frac{2}{\nu \hat{\mathcal{L}}} 
        - 
        \frac{\pi}{\nu^2 \hat{\mathcal{L}}^2} 
        \left[
           1 
           - 
           \mathrm{erfcx}
           \left(
           \frac{\nu \hat{\mathcal{L}}}{\sqrt{\pi}}
           \right)
        \right],
        \qquad
        \lambda_b L \gg 1,
    \end{equation}
\end{corollary}

where $\mathrm{erfcx}(\cdot)$ denotes the scaled complementary error function.
It follows that the Laplace transform of $d_{\rho}$ is an explicit function of $L$ and $\lambda_b$ given the asymptotic scaling $\hat{\mathcal{L}} \propto L^{\frac{1}{2}} \lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}}$ (Proposition \ref{proposition-diameter}).

\begin{proof}
    Consider a disturbance event randomly occurring along a given flow path.
    The full extent of this path, from its upstream to downstream endpoints, defines the network diameter $\mathcal{L}$.
    Assuming that habitats are randomly distributed along the path according to a Poisson point process, the distance from the upstream disturbance origin to a given downstream habitat is equivalent to the distance between two randomly selected points on a line segment of length $\mathcal{L}$.
    Therefore, conditional on the path length, the probability density function of the distance ($d_{\rho})$, denoted by $f(d_{\rho} \mid \mathcal{L})$, is

    \begin{equation}
        f(d_{\rho} \mid \mathcal{L}) = \frac{2}{\mathcal{L}}\left(1 - \frac{d_{\rho}}{\mathcal{L}}\right),
        \qquad (\because \text{Lemma \ref{lemma-d-rho-dist}}).
    \end{equation}
    
    The conditional Laplace transform given $\mathcal{L}$ is
    
    \begin{align}
        \begin{split}
            \mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp({-\nu d_{\rho}}) \mid \mathcal{L} \bigr)
            &= 
            \int_0^{\mathcal{L}} \exp({-\nu d_{\rho}}) f(d_{\rho} \mid \mathcal{L}) dd_{\rho}\\
            &= 
            \frac{2}{\nu \mathcal{L}}\bigl[1 - \exp(-\nu \mathcal{L})\bigr] -
            \frac{2}{\nu^2 \mathcal{L}^2}\bigl[1 - (1 + \nu \mathcal{L}) \exp(-\nu \mathcal{L}) \bigr]\\
            &= 
            \frac{2}{\nu \mathcal{L}}
            -
            \frac{2}{\nu^2 \mathcal{L}^2}
            +
            \frac{2}{\nu^2 \mathcal{L}^2} \exp(-\nu \mathcal{L})
            .    
        \end{split}
    \end{align}
    
    Under the assumption of random bifurcation, the distribution of $\mathcal{L}$ converges to a Rayleigh distribution in the regime $\lambda_b L \gg 1$ \citep{ojeda_distance_2022}.
    However, because disturbance events are assumed to occur uniformly per unit river length, longer flow paths have a proportionally higher probability of containing the disturbance origin. Therefore, the relevant distribution of path lengths is length-biased, with weighting function $w(\mathcal{L}) = \mathcal{L}/\hat{\mathcal{L}}$, where $\hat{\mathcal{{L}}} \equiv \mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L})$.
    With $\sigma$ denoting the scale parameter of the Rayleigh distribution, taking expectation over this length-biased distribution gives

    \begin{align}
        \begin{split}
            \mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp({-\nu d_{\rho}}) \bigr)
            &\approx
            \int_0^{\infty} 
                \mathbb{E}\bigl(\exp({-\nu d_{\rho}}) \mid \mathcal{L} \bigr) 
                \underbrace{
                w(\mathcal{L}) \frac{\mathcal{L}}{\sigma^2} \exp(-\frac{\mathcal{L}^2}{2 \sigma^2})
                }_{\text{Length-bias corrected Rayleigh}}
            d \mathcal{L}\\
            &= 
            \frac{2}{\nu \hat{\mathcal{L}}}
            \int_0^{\infty} 
                \frac{\mathcal{L}}{\sigma^2}
                \exp(-\frac{\mathcal{L}^2}{2 \sigma^2})
            d \mathcal{L} 
            -

\begin{lemma}
\label{lemma-d-rho-dist}
Consider a flow path of length $\mathcal{L}$, where disturbance origins and habitat locations are independently and uniformly distributed along the path. Conditional on a disturbance occurring upstream of a habitat, the distance $d_{\rho}$ between the disturbance origin and the habitat has probability density

\begin{equation}
    f(d_{\rho} \mid \mathcal{L})
    =
    \frac{2}{\mathcal{L}}
    \left(
        1-\frac{d_{\rho}}{\mathcal{L}}
    \right).
    \qquad
    0\leq d_{\rho} \leq \mathcal{L}.
\end{equation}

\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $X$ and $Y$ denote the locations of a disturbance origin and a habitat, respectively. 
Since both are uniformly distributed on $[0,\mathcal{L}]$, their joint probability density is uniform over the square $[0,\mathcal{L}]^2$. 
Conditioning on flow connectivity ($X<Y$, assuming larger coordinates correspond to downstream locations) restricts the distribution to the upper triangular region, yielding

\[
f_{X,Y}(x,y\mid X<Y)
=
\frac{2}{\mathcal{L}^2},
\qquad
0<x<y<\mathcal{L}.
\]

Let $d_{\rho} = Y-X$. 
Transforming variables from $(X,Y)$ to $(X,d_{\rho})$ and integrating over the allowable range $0<x<\mathcal{L}-d_{\rho}$ gives

\begin{equation}
    f(d_{\rho} \mid \mathcal{L})
    =
    \int_0^{\mathcal{L}-d_{\rho}}
    \frac{2}{\mathcal{L}^2}\,dx
    =
    \frac{2}{\mathcal{L}}
    \left(
    1-\frac{d_{\rho}}{\mathcal{L}}
    \right).
\end{equation}

\end{proof}

\subsection{Scaling of Key Geometric Properties} \label{updist}

In this section, we derive the expected values of network distance between habitats $d$, upstream river length $u$, and river network diameter $\mathcal{L}$ for a population of branching networks characterized by total river length $L$ and branching rate $\lambda_b$.
As we will show, these geometric properties asymptotically scale with $L$ and $\lambda_b$.
Our derivation accounts for all topologically distinct river networks; that is, we consider all possible arrangements of links that form a spanning tree, with each network structure properly weighted according to $L$ and $\lambda_b$.
Consequently, the derivation applies broadly to any river network with random bifurcations.

\begin{definition}
We use the following key terms: \textit{link} and \textit{magnitude}.
A link refers to a river segment from one confluence to another or to a terminal point of the river (i.e., the upstream origin or the river mouth; Figure \ref{fig:river-fig}).
Magnitude $m$ refers to the number of exterior source links (links containing upstream terminals; Figure \ref{fig:river-fig}) flowing into a given link within the network; thus, magnitude is defined for each link and $m=1$ at an exterior source link (Figure \ref{fig:river-fig}).
We denote by $M$ the network-wide magnitude, which corresponds to the magnitude of the outlet link.

We denote by $M$ the \textit{network-wide magnitude}, defined as the magnitude of the outlet link.     \label{fig:river-fig}
\end{SCfigure}

\noindent\makebox[\linewidth]{\rule{\textwidth}{0.4pt}}

We assume that the length of individual link $s$, denoted $l_s$, follows an exponential distribution $l_s \sim \mbox{Exp}(\lambda_b)$ with the constraint $\sum_{s = 1}^{B} l_s = L$ ($B$ denotes the number of links).
The length $l_s$ can be interpreted as the ``waiting time'' to a branching event (or termination events for upstream sources), such that the number of branching events $Z$ within the given length $L$ follows a Poisson distribution.
The total number of links $B$ is given by $Z+1$, where the additional link accounts for the final termination event at network length $L$.
Because a bifurcating network requires an odd number of links, $Z$ must be even, ensuring that $B$ satisfies this topological constraint.
Consequently, $Z$ follows a truncated Poisson distribution as
$Z \sim \mathrm{Pois}(\lambda_b L)$ conditioned on $Z \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$ ($2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$ denotes the set of even integers).
We use these assumptions in the following derivation.

\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
\label{proposition-mu-d}
Consider a population of random branching networks with total river length $L$ and branching rate $\lambda_b$.
Let $d$ denote the network distance between two random points $i$ and $j$ selected uniformly at random, and define

\begin{equation}
    \hat{d} \equiv \mathbb{E}\bigl(d(i, j)\bigr).
\end{equation}

Then the expected pairwise distance asymptotically exhibits a scaling relationship with total river length and branching rate,

\begin{equation}
    \hat{d}
    \propto
    L^{\frac{1}{2}}\lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}},
    \qquad
    \lambda_b L \to \infty.
\end{equation}

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We decompose the distance between two habitats (random points), denoted by $d(i,j)$ along a network, into two cases: (i) both habitats lie on the same link (``within''), and (ii) the habitats are located on different links (``between'').
We denote the corresponding distances by $d_w(i,j)$ and $d_b(i,j)$, respectively.
The expected distance between two habitats given $z$ (the number of links minus one) can then be written as

\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}\bigl(d(i,j\mid z)\bigr)
=
\mathbb{E}_{\{l\}\mid z}
\left[
\Pr \bigl(w \mid \{l\} \bigr)\, d_w(i,j \mid \{l\})
+
\bigl(1 - \Pr(w \mid \{l\})\bigr)\, d_b(i,j \mid z)
\right],
\label{eq:mu-d-z}
\end{equation}

where $\Pr \bigl(w \mid \{l\} \bigr)$ denotes the probability that two habitats are located on the same link, and $\cdot \mid \{l\}$ indicates the conditioning on a realization of link lengths $\{l\} = \{l_1, l_2, ..., l_{z+1}\}$.

The within-link distance, $d_w(i,j \mid \{l\})$, can be derived from the expected distance between two points chosen uniformly at random on a line segment. 
For a link of length $l_s$, this expected distance is $3^{-1}\,l_s$.
In our framework, however, link lengths are random and distributed as exponential variables subject to the constraint $\sum_s l_s = L$. 
Conditioning on the event that both points fall in the same but any link, the probability that two random points lies on link $s$ is

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \Pr\bigl(\exists s: i, j\in s\mid\{l\} \bigr)
    &= 
    \left(
        \frac{l_s}{L}
    \right)^{2} 
    \left[
        \sum_{k=1}^{z+1} \left( \frac{l_k}{L}\right)^2
    \right]^{-1}\\
    &= 
    F_s^{2} 
    \left(
        \sum_{k=1}^{z+1} F_k^2
    \right)^{-1}\\
    &= 
    F_s^{2} 
    \bigl[
        \Pr\bigl(w \mid \{l\}\bigr)
    \bigr]^{-1},
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where $F_s = l_s L^{-1}$ and $\Pr(w \mid \{l\}) = \sum_{k=1}^{z+1} F_k^2$.
Thus, this conditional probability induces a length-biased weighting proportional to $l_s^2$ since two independent uniform points are more likely to fall on longer links.
It follows that

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \Pr \bigl(w \mid \{l\}\bigr) d_w(i,j \mid \{l\})
    &= 
    \Pr \bigl(w \mid \{l\} \bigr) \sum_{s=1}^{z+1} \frac{l_s}{3}\,
    \Pr \bigl(i \in s, j \in s \mid \{l\}\bigr) \\
    &=
    \Pr \bigl(w \mid \{l\} \bigr) \sum_{s=1}^{z+1} \frac{l_s}{3}\, F_s^2 
    \bigl[\Pr \bigl(w \mid \{l\} \bigr) \bigr]^{-1} \\
    &= 
    \frac{L}{3}\,
    \sum_{s=1}^{z+1} F_s^3.
    \end{split}
\end{align}

Taking expectation over the conditional distribution of link lengths yields

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(d_w(i,j \mid z) \bigr) 
    &\equiv 
    \mathbb{E}_{\{l\}|z} \bigl[\Pr \bigl(w \mid \{l\} \bigr)\, d_w(i,j \mid \{l\})\bigr] \\
    &= 
    \frac{L}{3} \sum_{s = 1}^{z+1}\mathbb{E}\bigl(F_s^3\bigr)\\
    &=\frac{L}{3} \cdot \frac{6(z+1)}{(z+1)(z+2)(z+3)}\\
    &= \frac{2L}{(z+2)(z+3)}\\
    &= L \left[ 2z^{-2} + \mathcal{O}(z^{-3}) \right],
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where we used the known third moment of a Dirichlet distribution for $\mathbb{E}\bigl(F_s^3\bigr)$ as the set of fractional link lengths $(F_1, F_2,...,F_{z+1})$ follows a Dirichlet distribution (by Lemma \ref{lemma-f}).

The between-link distance, $d_b(i,j \mid z)$, can be derived as the product of the topological distance between links and the mean link length.
We denote $d_b(i,j \mid z)$ without conditioning on a realization of link lengths $\{l\}$ because $d_b(i,j \mid z)$ is deterministic once $z$ is given.

Let $d'_b(s,t \mid z)$ be the topological distance between links, defined as the number of intervening links that must be traversed to go from link $s$ to link $t$ (i.e., the count of links excluding $s$, but including all subsequent links up to $t$).
The topological distance given $z$ ($z \ge 2$) can be written as

\begin{equation}
d'_b(s, t \mid z)
=
\sum_{a \in \mathcal{E}}
\mathbf{1}\{a \in \mathrm{path}(s,t)\},
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{E}$ denotes the set of all links in the network, and $\mathbf{1}\{\cdot\}$ is the indicator function.
We conditioned on $z \ge 2$ (the number of links $b \ge 3$) so that we can choose a link pair to define the topological distance.

Summing over all pairs yields

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \sum_{s < t} d'_b(s, t \mid z)
    &= \sum_{s < t} \sum_{a \in \mathcal{E}} \mathbf{1}\{a \in \mathrm{path}(s,t)\} \\
    &= \sum_{a \in \mathcal{E}} \sum_{s < t} \mathbf{1}\{a \in \mathrm{path}(s,t)\},
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where we exchanged the order of summation.
For each link $a$, the inner sum counts the number of link pairs whose unique path traverses link $a$, equivalently the number of link pairs separated by the removal of the link $a$.

In a bifurcating branching network of magnitude $M$, the total number of links is $2M - 1$.
For any link $a$, its removal partition the network into subnetworks of magnitudes $m$ and $M - m$.
The number of link pairs whose unique path traverses $a$ is therefore equal to the product of the number of links in each resulting component, i.e., $(2m - 1)(2M - 2m)$.
It follows that

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \sum_{s < t} d'_b(s, t \mid z)
    &= \sum_{a \in \mathcal{E}} \sum_{s < t} \mathbf{1}\{a \in \mathrm{path}(s,t)\}\\
    &= \sum_{m = 1}^M B(m) (2m - 1) (2M - 2m),
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where $m$ denotes the magnitude of a subnetwork induced by a link within a $M$-magnitude branching network, and $B(m)$ is the number of links whose magnitude is $m$.
Assuming that link pairs are uniformly sampled from all possible combinations $C_b = (2M-1)(2M-2)/2$, taking expectation over the distribution of topologically distinct river networks given $M$ obtains

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(d'_b(s, t \mid z) \bigr) 
    &= \frac{\mathbb{E} \bigl(\sum_{m=1}^M B(m)(2m - 1) (2M - 2m)\bigr)}{C_b}\\
    &= \frac{\sum_{m=1}^M (2M-1)w(m;M)(2m - 1) (2M - 2m)}{C_b}\\
    &= \frac{(4M + 2) \mathbb{E}(m) - 4 \mathbb{E}(m^2) - 2M}{M-1}\\
    &= \frac{M}{M-1}\left[4^M {\binom{2M}{M}}^{-1} - 2\right] \qquad (\because \text{Lemmas \ref{lemma-m} and \ref{lemma-m2}})\\
    &= \frac{z+2}{z}\left[2^{z+2} \binom{z+2}{\frac{z+2}{2}}^{-1} - 2\right],
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where we used the topological relationship $M = 2^{-1}(z + 2)$ to condition on $z$.

The topological distance $\mathbb{E}\bigl(d'_b(s, t \mid z)\bigr)$ has units of links.
Converting this quantity into the network distance between two random points $i$ and $j$, $d_b(i, j \mid z)$, requires accounting for the lengths of individual links.
Under the assumption that link lengths are independent of the network topology, the expected distance decomposes as the product of the mean link length $\hat{l} = L(z+1)^{-1}$ (Equation \ref{eq:l-hat}) and the expected topological distance.
We yield

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(d_b(i, j \mid z)\bigr) 
    & =
    \mathbb{E}_{\{l\} \mid z}\bigl(d_b(i, j \mid z)\bigr)\\
    &= 
    \hat{l}~\mathbb{E}\bigl(d'_b(s, t \mid z) \bigr)\\
    &= 
    \frac{L(z+2)}{z (z + 1)}\left[2^{z+2}  \binom{z+2}{\frac{z+2}{2}}^{-1} - 2\right].
    \end{split}
\end{align}

Using Stirling's approximation of the central binomial coefficient, we yield

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(d_b(i, j \mid z) \bigr) 
    &= \frac{L(z+2)}{z(z + 1)}\left[\left( \frac{\pi(z+2)}{2} \right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \left(1 + \frac{1}{4(z+2)} + \mathcal{O}(z^{-2})\right) - 2\right]\\
    &= L\left[C z^{-\frac{1}{2}} - 2z^{-1} + \mathcal{O}(z^{-\frac{3}{2}})\right] \qquad (\because \text{Taylor expansion}).
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where $C = \sqrt{\pi/2}$.

Substituting $\mathbb{E}\bigl(d_w(i,j \mid z)\bigr)$ and $\mathbb{E}\bigl(d_b(i,j \mid z)\bigr)$ into Equation \ref{eq:mu-d-z} yields

\begin{align}
\begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(d(i,j \mid z)\bigr) 
    &= 
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(d_w(i,j \mid z) \bigr) + \bigl[1 - \mathbb{E}_{\{l\} \mid z}\bigl(\Pr(w \mid \{l\})\bigr) \bigr] \mathbb{E}\bigl(d_b(i,j \mid z) \bigr)\\
    &= 
    L\left[2z^{-2} + \mathcal{O}(z^{-3})\right] +
    \frac{z}{z+2} L\left[C z^{-\frac{1}{2}} - 2z^{-1} + \mathcal{O}(z^{-\frac{3}{2}})\right]\\
    &= 
    L \left[Cz^{-\frac{1}{2}} - 2z^{-1}  + \mathcal{O}(z^{-\frac{3}{2}}) \right],
\end{split}
\end{align}

where 

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}_{\{l\} \mid z}\bigl(\Pr(w \mid \{l\})\bigr) 
    = 
    \sum_{s = 1}^{z+1} \mathbb{E}\bigl(F_s^2\bigr) 
    = 
    \frac{2}{z + 2},
\end{equation}

given the known second moment of a Dirichlet distribution (by Lemma \ref{lemma-f}).

Recall that $z$ is a realization of the random variable $Z$, which obeys a truncated Poisson distribution as $Z \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_b L)$ conditioned on $Z \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$.
The exact closure is:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}\bigl(d(i,j)\bigr) = \sum_{z \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}} \mathbb{E}\bigl(d(i,j \mid z)\bigr) f'_{\text{pois}}(z; L, \lambda_b),
\end{equation}

where $f'_{\text{pois}}(z; L, \lambda_b)$ denotes the probability mass function for the truncated Poisson distribution (Lemma \ref{lemma-pois}).
For $\lambda_bL \gg 1$, applying the delta method obtains

\begin{equation}
\hat{d} \equiv \mathbb{E}\bigl(d(i, j)\bigr) = C\left(\frac{L}{\lambda_b}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} - \frac{2}{\lambda_b} +  \mathcal{O}\bigl((\lambda_b L)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\bigr),
\end{equation}

It follows that

\begin{equation}
    \hat{d}
    \propto
    L^{\frac{1}{2}}\lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}},
    \qquad
    \lambda_b L \to \infty.
\end{equation}

\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}
\label{proposition-u}
Consider a population of random branching networks with total river length $L$ and branching rate $\lambda_b$.
Let $u_i$ denote the upstream river length at a random location on a network, and define

\begin{equation}
    \hat{u} \equiv \mathbb{E}(u).
\end{equation}

Then the expected upstream river length asymptotically exhibits a scaling relationship with total river length and branching rate,

\begin{equation}
\hat{u} \propto L^{\frac{1}{2}} \lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}}, \qquad \lambda_bL \rightarrow \infty.
\end{equation}

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The expected upstream river length, $\mathbb{E}(u)$, can be decomposed as $\mathbb{E}(u) = \mathbb{E}(u') + \mathbb{E}(l'_{s[i]})$, where $u'$ is the summed length of upstream links (excluding the link containing the habitat) and $l'_{s[i]}$ is the distance from the upstream link end to habitat $i$ in link $s$.

The summed length of upstream links $u'$ is the product of the mean length of a single link $\hat{l}$ and the number of upstream links $n$.
With the known sum $\sum_{s=1}^b l_s = L$, the fraction of single link length $F_s$ (= $l_s L^{-1}$) follows a Beta distribution as $F_s \mid z \sim \mbox{Beta}(1, z)$ (Lemma \ref{lemma-f}), whose expected value is $\mathbb{E}(F_s\mid z) = (z + 1)^{-1}$.
Thus, we obtain an intuitive solution for the expectation of mean length as

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \hat{l} \equiv \mathbb{E}(l_s\mid z) &= L \cdot \mathbb{E}(F_s\mid z)\\
                                      &= L(z + 1)^{-1}.
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:l-hat}
\end{align}

In the limit of $\lambda_b L \rightarrow \infty$, the expected value of this quantity, $\mathbb{E}(LB^{-1})$, approaches $\lambda_b^{-1}$.

The number of upstream links $n$ requires the consideration of its probability distribution since $n$ depends on the location in a network.
Here, our approach leverages the known properties of Shreve's topologically distinct river networks \cite{shreve_infinite_1967}, a classical framework of bifurcating river networks.
The probability of drawing a link with magnitude $m$ at random from a population of $M$-magnitude topologically distinct river networks, $\omega(m; M)$, is

\begin{equation}
    \omega(m; M) = \frac{1}{2m - 1} \binom{2m}{m} \binom{2(M - m)}{M - m} \binom{2M}{M}^{-1}.
    \label{eq:m-pmf}
\end{equation}

The notation $\binom{\cdot}{\cdot}$ and $\binom{\cdot}{\cdot}^{-1}$ denote a binomial coefficient and its inverse.
The number of upstream links $n$ has a linear relationship with link magnitude $m$ as $n = 2m - 2$ due to the bifurcating nature of topologically distinct river networks.
Using the expected value of $m$ ($\sum_{m=1}^{M} m~\omega(m; M)$; Lemma \ref{lemma-m}), we obtain

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}(n\mid z) 
        &= \sum_{m=1}^{M} (2m - 2) \omega(m; M)\\
        &= 2 \sum_{m=1}^{M} m~\omega(m; M) - 2\\
        &= 4^{M} \binom{2M}{M}^{-1} - 2.
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:n-hat}
\end{align}

Topologically distinct river networks satisfy the relationship $b = 2M - 1$ because two source links must join to form a single downstream link \cite{shreve_infinite_1967, rodriguez-iturbe_fractal_2001}.
This means that $M$ is a function of $z$: $M = (b + 1) / 2 = (z + 2) / 2$.
The expected value $\mathbb{E}(n\mid z)$ can be rewritten as a function of $z$:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{n}(z)
    \equiv \mathbb{E}(n\mid z)
    = 2^{z + 2} \binom{z + 2}{\frac{z + 2}{2}}^{-1} - 2.
\end{equation}

Since link lengths are independent of the network topology, we obtain $\mathbb{E}(u' \mid z)$ as the product of the mean link length and the number of upstream links

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{E}(u' \mid z) 
        &= \hat{l}~\hat{n}(z).
        % \\
        % &= \frac{L}{z + 1} \hat{n}(z).
    \end{split}
\end{align}

The expected distance from the upstream link end to a given habitat, $\mathbb{E}(l'_{s[i]}\mid z)$, is calculated as follows.
Assuming that habitats are randomly distributed along the river (i.e., Poisson process), the distance to habitat $i$ in link $s$, $l'_{s[i]}$, follows a uniform distribution, i.e.,  $\Pr(l'_{s[i]}\mid z, l_s) = \mbox{Unif}(0, l_s)$.
Thus, the conditional expectation of the distance is

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(l'_{s[i]}\mid z, l_s) = \frac{l_s}{2}.
\end{equation}

% Now, we derive the expected distance to a habitat in a focal link $\mathbb{E}(l'_{s[i]}\mid z)$.
Recall that the conditional probability of the distance to a given habitat is $\Pr(l'_{s[i]}\mid z)= \int \Pr(l'_{s[i]}\mid z, l_s) \Pr(l_s\mid z)d l_s$, and that the expected length of link $s$ is $\mathbb{E}(l_s\mid z) = L(z+1)^{-1}$.
Then, we obtain 

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{E}(l'_{s[i]}\mid z)
        &= \int l'_{s[i]} \Pr(l'_{s[i]}\mid z) dl'_{s[i]}\\
        &= \iint l'_{s[i]} \Pr(l'_{s[i]}\mid z, l_s) \Pr(l_s\mid z) dl'_{s[i]} dl_s \\
        &= \frac{1}{2} \int l_s \Pr(l_s\mid z) dl_s \\
        &= \frac{\hat{l}}{2}.
    \end{split}
\end{align}

Summing $\mathbb{E}(u' \mid z)$ and $\mathbb{E}(l'_{s[i]}\mid z)$ with a given value of $z$ yields

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        \mathbb{E}(u \mid z) 
        &= \mathbb{E}(u' \mid z) + \mathbb{E}(l'_{s[i]}\mid z)\\
        &= \hat{l} \left[ \hat{n}(z) + \frac{1}{2} \right]\\
        &= \frac{L}{z+1} \left[ \hat{n}(z) + \frac{1}{2} \right].
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:cu}
\end{align}

Using Stirling's approximation for the central binomial coefficient of $E(u\mid z)$, we obtain

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    E(u\mid z) 
    &= \frac{L}{z + 1}\left[\underbrace{\left\{\frac{\pi(z + 2)}{2}\right\}^{\frac{1}{2}}\left\{1 + \frac{1}{4(z + 2)} + \mathcal{O}(z^{-2})\right\} - 2}_{\text{Stirling's approximation of }\hat{n}(z)} + \frac{1}{2}\right]\\
    &= L \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \left(z^{-\frac{1}{2}} + \frac{1}{4}z^{-\frac{3}{2}}\right)-\frac{3L}{2}(z^{-1} - z^{-2}) + \mathcal{O}(z^{-\frac{5}{2}}) \qquad (\because \text{Taylor expansion}).
    \end{split}
\end{align}

Recall that $z$ is a realization of the random variable $Z$, which obeys a truncated Poisson distribution as $Z \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_b L)$ conditioned on $Z \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$.
The exact closure is:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(u) = \sum_{z \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}} \mathbb{E}(u\mid z) f'_{\text{pois}}(z; L, \lambda_b),
\end{equation}

where $f'_{\text{pois}}(z; L, \lambda_b)$ denotes the probability mass function for the truncated Poisson distribution (Lemma \ref{lemma-pois}).
For $\lambda_b L \gg 1$, applying the delta method yields

\begin{equation}
    \hat{u} \equiv \mathbb{E}(u) = C \left({\frac{L}{\lambda_b}}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}} - \frac{3}{2 \lambda_b} + \mathcal{O}((\lambda_b L)^{-\frac{1}{2}}),
\end{equation}

where $C = \sqrt{\pi/2}$.
It follows that

\begin{equation}
    \hat{u}
    \propto
    L^{\frac{1}{2}}\lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}},
    \qquad
    \lambda_b L \to \infty.
\end{equation}

\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}
\label{proposition-diameter}
Consider a population of random branching networks with total river length $L$ and branching rate $\lambda_b$.
Let $\mathcal{L}$ denote the network diameter, i.e., the network length from the outlet to a given upstream termination point, and define

\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathcal{L}} \equiv \mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L}).
\end{equation}

Then the expected diameter asymptotically exhibits a scaling relationship with total river length and branching rate,

\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathcal{L}}
    \propto
    L^{\frac{1}{2}}\lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}},
    \qquad
    \lambda_b L \to \infty.
\end{equation}

\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $\mathcal{L}_{v}'$ denote the topological length from the outlet link $o$ to exterior source link $v$, defined as the number of links on the unique path connecting them, including both the outlet and source links.
The topological length can be written as

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{v}'
=
\sum_{a \in \mathcal{E}}
\mathbf{1}\{a \in \mathrm{path}(o,v)\},
\end{equation}

where $\mathcal{E}$ denotes the set of all links in the network, and $\mathbf{1}\{\cdot\}$ is the indicator function.
Summing over all exterior source links ($=M$) yields

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \sum_{v = 1}^M \mathcal{L}_{v}'
    &= \sum_{v = 1}^M \sum_{a \in \mathcal{E}} \mathbf{1}\{a \in \mathrm{path}(o,v)\} \\
    &= \sum_{a \in \mathcal{E}} \sum_{v = 1}^M \mathbf{1}\{a \in \mathrm{path}(o,v)\},
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where we exchanged the order of summation.
For each link $a$, the inner sum counts the number of exterior source links whose unique path to the outlet traverses $a$. This quantity is precisely the magnitude of link $a$, denoted by $m_a$. Therefore,

\begin{equation}
\sum_{v=1}^{M} \mathcal{L}_{v}' = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{E}} m_a.
\end{equation}

Since the total contribution can be grouped by link magnitude, the right-hand side can be rewritten as

\begin{equation}
\sum_{a\in\mathcal{E}} m_a = \sum_{m=1}^{M} m B(m),
\end{equation}

where $B(m)$ denotes the number of links of magnitude $m$.
Consequently, the expected topological network diameter is

\begin{align}
\begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L}' \mid z) 
    &= \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} m~\mathbb{E}(B(m))\\
    &= \frac{2M - 1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} m~\omega(m; M)\\
    &= \frac{2M - 1}{M} \mathbb{E}(m)\\
    &= \frac{2M - 1}{2M} \cdot 4^{M} \binom{2M}{M}^{-1} \qquad (\because \text{Lemma \ref{lemma-m}})\\
    &= \frac{z+1}{z+2} \cdot 2^{z+2} {\binom{z+2}{\frac{z + 2}{2}}}^{-1},
\end{split}
\end{align}

where $\omega(m;M)$ is the probability mass function for link magnitude $m$ (Equation \ref{eq:m-pmf}) and we used the topological relationship $M = 2^{-1}(z + 2)$.

The quantity $\mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L}'\mid z)$ has units of links.
To convert this quantity into a network distance, link lengths must be taken into account. 
Assuming that link lengths are independent of network topology, the expected network diameter is given by the product of the mean link length $\hat{l} = L(z+1)^{-1}$ (Equation \ref{eq:l-hat}) and the expected topological diameter.
As such

\begin{align}
\begin{split} 
    \mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L} \mid z) 
    &= \hat{l}~\mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L}' \mid z)\\
    &= \frac{L}{z+2} \cdot 2^{z + 2} {\binom{z+2}{\frac{z + 2}{2}}}^{-1}\\
    &= \frac{L}{z+2} \left[\frac{\pi(z + 2)}{2}\right]^{\frac{1}{2}} \left[1 + \frac{1}{4 (z + 2)}  + \mathcal{O}(z^{-2})\right] \qquad (\because \text{Stirling's approximation})\\
    &= L \left(\frac{\pi}{2} \right)^{\frac{1}{2}} \left[z^{-\frac{1}{2}} - \frac{3}{4} z^{-\frac{3}{2}} + \mathcal{O}(z^{-\frac{5}{2}}) \right] \qquad (\because \text{Taylor expansion})
\end{split} 
\end{align}

Recall that $z$ is a realization of the random variable $Z$, which obeys a truncated Poisson distribution as $Z \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_b L)$ conditioned on $Z \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge0}$.
The exact closure is:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L})  = \sum_{z \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}} \mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L} \mid z) f'_{\text{pois}}(z; L, \lambda_b),
\end{equation}

where $f'_{\text{pois}}(z; L, \lambda_b)$ denotes the probability mass function for the truncated Poisson distribution (Lemma \ref{lemma-pois}).
For $\lambda_b L \gg 1$, applying the delta method obtains

\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathcal{L}} 
\equiv 
\mathbb{E}(\mathcal{L}) 
= 
C \left(\frac{L}{\lambda_b} \right)^{\frac{1}{2}} + \mathcal{O}\bigl((\lambda_b L)^{-\frac{1}{2}}\bigr),
\end{equation}

where $C = \sqrt{\pi / 2}$.
It follows that

\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathcal{L}}
    \propto
    L^{\frac{1}{2}}\lambda_b^{-\frac{1}{2}},
    \qquad
    \lambda_b L \to \infty.
\end{equation}
    
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lemma-f}
Let link length $l_1, l_2, \ldots, l_{z+1}$ be independent random variables, each following $\mbox{Exp}(\lambda_b)$, where $z+1$ is the number of links in the network.
If the total length is given as $\sum_{s=1}^{z+1} l_s = L$, the fraction of single link length $F_s = l_s L^{-1}$ follows a Beta distribution $\mbox{Beta}(1, z)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
An exponential distribution $\mbox{Exp}(\lambda_b)$ is a special case of Gamma distribution, i.e., $\mbox{Ga}(1, \lambda_b)$.
Thus, $l_1, l_2, \ldots, l_{z+1}$  follow $\mbox{Ga}(1, \lambda_b)$.
Let us denote $X_1 = l_s$, $X_2 = \sum_{s'} l_{s'}$ ($s' \in \{1,2, \ldots, z+1\} \setminus \{s\}$), and $X_1 + X_2 = Y$.
Given the reproductive property of Gamma distribution, $X_2 \sim \mbox{Ga}(z, \lambda_b)$ and $Y \sim \mbox{Ga}(z + 1, \lambda_b)$.
The conditional probability density of $X_1 = x$ ($0 \le x \le L$) given $Y = L$, denoted as $f_{X_1}(X_1 = x\mid Y = L)$, is

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        f_{X_1}(X_1 = x\mid Y = L)
        &= \frac{f_{X_1}(X_1 = x\mid Y = L) f_{X_2}(X_2 = L - x\mid Y = L)}{f_Y(Y = L)}\\
        &= 
        \left[
        \frac{\lambda_b^{1 + z}}{\Gamma(1) \Gamma(z)} (L - x)^{z - 1} e^{-\lambda_b L}
        \right]
        \left[
        \frac{\lambda_b^{1 + z}}{\Gamma(1 + z)} L^{z} e^{-\lambda_b L}
        \right]^{-1}\\
        &= \frac{\Gamma(1 + z)}{\Gamma(1) \Gamma(z) L^{z}} (L - x)^{z - 1}.
    \end{split}
    \label{eq:beta}
\end{align}

Equation \ref{eq:beta} indicates that $f_{X_1}(X_1 = x\mid Y = 1)$ is the probability density function of $\mbox{Beta}(1, z)$.
We obtain

\begin{equation}
    F_s=\frac{l_s}{L} \sim \mbox{Beta} (1, z).
\end{equation}

\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lemma-pois}
Let $X ~ \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$.
Define the conditional random variable $Y = X \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}~(X~\text{is even})$.
Then $Y$ takes values in $\{0, 2, 4,...\}$ with probability mass function

\begin{equation}
    f'_{\text{pois}}(Y;\lambda) = \Pr(Y = 2n) = \frac{\lambda^{2n}}{(2n)!\cosh(\lambda)}, n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(X \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)\), so that
\begin{equation}
    \Pr(X=n) = \frac{\lambda^n e^{-\lambda}}{n!}.
\end{equation}

Define the conditional random variable \(Y = X \mid (X \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0})\), i.e., \(Y\) is \(X\) conditioned on being even.

Then for \(n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}\),
\begin{equation}
    \Pr(Y = 2n)
    = \frac{\Pr(X=2n)}{\Pr(X \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0})}.
\end{equation}

Using the probability mass function of a Poisson distribution,

\begin{equation}
    \Pr(X=2n) = \frac{\lambda^{2n} e^{-\lambda}}{(2n)!}.
\end{equation}

Next, the probability of an even outcome is
\begin{equation}
    \Pr(X \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0})
    = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{\lambda^{2n} e^{-\lambda}}{(2n)!}
    = e^{-\lambda} \cosh(\lambda).
\end{equation}

Therefore,
\begin{equation}
    \Pr(Y=2n)
    = \frac{\lambda^{2n}}{(2n)! \cosh(\lambda)}.
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lemma-m}
Let $\omega(m; M)$ be a probability mass function defined as

\begin{equation}
    \omega(m; M) = \frac{1}{2m - 1} \binom{2m}{m} \binom{2(M - m)}{M - m} \binom{2M}{M}^{-1}.
\end{equation}

Then, the expected value of $m$ is given by

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(m; M) = \sum_{m = 1}^M m~\omega(m; M) = \frac{4^{M}}{2}\binom{2M}{M}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We begin by reorganizing the probability mass function

\begin{equation}
    \omega(m; M) = \frac{(2M - 2m - 1) M}{(M - m)(2M - 1)} \omega(m; M - 1).
\end{equation}

From this, we can express

\begin{equation}
    (M - m) \omega(m; M) = \frac{(2M - 2m - 1) M}{2M - 1} \omega(m; M - 1).
\end{equation}

Summing over all $m$, we get

\begin{equation}
    \sum_{m = 1}^{M - 1} (M - m) \omega(m; M)
    = \sum_{m = 1}^{M} (M - m) \omega(m; M)
    = \frac{M}{2M - 1} \sum_{m = 1}^{M - 1} (2M - 2m - 1) \omega(m; M - 1).
\end{equation}

Using the fact that $\sum_{m = 1}^{M} \omega(m; M) = 1$, the equation above leads to the following recurrence relation for the expected value:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(m; M) = \frac{2M}{2M - 1} \mathbb{E}(m; M - 1).
\end{equation}

Considering $\mathbb{E}(m; M = 1) = 1$, we can recursively calculate the expected value:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(m; M) = \frac{2M}{2M - 1} \cdot \frac{2M - 2}{2M - 3} \cdot \ldots \cdot \frac{4}{3} \cdot \mathbb{E}(m; 1) = 4^{M - 1} \frac{M!(M - 1)!}{(2M - 1)!} = \frac{4^{M}}{2}\frac{M!M!}{(2M)!}.
\end{equation}

We obtain

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(m; M) = \frac{4^{M}}{2}\binom{2M}{M}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}
\label{lemma-m2}
Let $\omega(m; M)$ be a probability mass function defined as

\begin{equation}
    \omega(m; M) = \frac{1}{2m - 1} \binom{2m}{m} \binom{2(M - m)}{M - m} \binom{2M}{M}^{-1}.
\end{equation}

Then, the expected value of $m^2$ is given by

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb{E}(m^2; M) = \frac{4^M (M + 1)}{4} {\binom{2M}{M}}^{-1}.
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}(m^2) 
    &= \sum_{m = 1}^M m^2~\omega(m; M) \\
    &= \sum_{m = 1}^M m^2 \frac{1}{2m - 1} \binom{2m}{m} \binom{2(M - m)}{M - m} \binom{2M}{M}^{-1}\\
    &= 2 \binom{2M}{M}^{-1} \sum_{m = 1}^M m \binom{2m - 2}{m - 1} \binom{2M-2m}{M-m}.
    \end{split}
\end{align}

Let $k = m-1$, $n = M-1$, and $C = 2 \binom{2M}{M}^{-1}$. 
We obtain

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    \mathbb{E}(m^2) 
    &= C \sum_{k=0}^n (k + 1) \binom{2k}{k} \binom{2n - 2k}{n - k} \\
    &= C \left[\sum_{k=0}^n k \binom{2k}{k} \binom{2n - 2k}{n - k} + \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{2k}{k} \binom{2n - 2k}{n - k}\right]\\
    & = C(\frac{4^nn}{2} + 4^n) \qquad (\because \text{the Cauchy convolution of central binomial coefficients})\\
    &= 4^n (n + 2) {\binom{2M}{M}}^{-1}\\
    &= \frac{4^M (M + 1)}{4} {\binom{2M}{M}}^{-1}.
    \end{split}
\end{align}

\end{proof}

\subsection{Uniqueness of Stable Equilibrium}
 
\begin{proposition}
Under the condition that $\mu_k^{(2)} = 0$, the system given by Equation \ref{eq:master} has a unique locally stable equilibrium.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The fraction of habitats occupied by species $k$ at equilibrium is written as follows:

\begin{align}
    \frac{dp_k}{dt} = 0 \Leftrightarrow p_k = 
         0 ~\text{or}~
         p_{k}^{*},
\end{align}

where $p_{k}^{*} = 1 - \mu_k / \gamma_k$.
Note that $p_{k}^{*} <0 \Leftrightarrow \gamma_k-\mu_k<0$ is biologically meaningless.

Under the preferential prey model, species $k$ may consume a subset of species $1,2,\ldots, k-1$ but not $k+1, k+2, \ldots, S$ because the food web is acyclic (see Methods in the main text).
This implies that $\mu_k$ and $\gamma_k$ depend on at least one of $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_{k-1}$, but are independent of $p_{k+1}, p_{k+2}, \ldots, p_{S}$.

The Jacobian matrix of Equation \ref{eq:master}, denoted as $J$, is then lower-triangular.
The characteristic equation of this Jacobian matrix at equilibrium is given as:

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        &|\lambda I - J| = 0\\
        \Leftrightarrow 
        &\prod_{k=1}^{S}(\lambda -J_{kk})=0\\
        \Leftrightarrow
        &\lambda = J_{kk}~ \forall k,
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where $I$ and $\lambda$ are the identity matrix and the eigenvalue, respectively, and 

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
        J_{kk}  &= \gamma_k(1-2p_k)-\mu_k \\
                &=\left\{
                       \begin{array}{cl}
                       \mu_k - \gamma_k& \text{if}~ p_k=p_k^*\\
                       \gamma_k - \mu_k& \text{if}~ p_k=0.
                       \end{array}
                   \right.
    \end{split}
\end{align}

An equilibrium is locally stable if and only if all eigenvalues $\lambda$ are negative. Therefore, a focal equilibrium is locally stable if and only if

\begin{align}
    \lambda < 0 &\Leftrightarrow 
    \left\{
    \begin{array}{cc}
         \mu_k - \gamma_k < 0   &\text{if}~ p_k = p_k^*\\
         \gamma_k - \mu_k < 0   &\text{if}~ p_k = 0.
    \end{array}
    \right.
    \label{eq:eigenvalue}
\end{align}

Equation \ref{eq:eigenvalue} indicates that $\mu_k - \gamma_k < 0$ for persistent species ($p_k = p_k^*>0$) and $\gamma_k - \mu_k < 0$ for extinct species ($p_k=0$) at a stable equilibrium.

Now, we demonstrate the uniqueness of the stable equilibrium, despite the system having multiple equilibria due to the various permutational combinations of persistent and extinct species.
For producer species ($k=1,2,\ldots, P$), $\gamma_k-\mu_k$ is constant as follows:

\begin{align}
    \gamma_k-\mu_k = r_0 c_{0, k} - \mu_{k}^{(0)} (1 + \rho \hat{u}).
\end{align}

This indicates the unique combination of the persistent and extinct producers at a stable equilibrium.
For consumer species ($k = P+1, P+2,\ldots, S$), $\gamma_k-\mu_k$ depends on $p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_{k-1}$ as follows:

\begin{align}
    \gamma_k-\mu_k = \frac{\left(c_{0, k}+\mu_{k}^{(1)}\right)}{|A_k|}\sum_{q \in A_k} p_q
- \mu_{k}^{(0)} (1 + \rho \hat{u})-\mu_{k}^{(1)} ,
\end{align}

where $A_k$ is the set of consumable prey for species $k$.

Recall that $A_k$ does not include species $q = k, k+1, \ldots, S$ due to the acyclic nature of the food web.
For species $k = P+1$, the value of $\mu_k-\gamma_k$ at a stable equilibrium is unique because the producer's occupancy at a stable equilibrium ($p_1, p_2, \ldots, p_P$) is unique (i.e., $\sum_{q \in A_k} p_q$ is unique).
In other words, the persistence state of the producer species at a stable equilibrium uniquely determines the state of species $k = P+1$ at a stable equilibrium.
This logic recursively applies to species $k=P+2. \ldots, S$.
Therefore, the system given by Equation \ref{eq:master} has a unique stable equilibrium.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Discharge-Precipitation Relationship}
We used daily river discharge data from 2000 to 2015, simulated by the Global Hydrodynamics model \textit{CaMa-Flood}, to estimate flow variability  \cite{yamazaki_deriving_2009, kimura_methodology_2023, lin_global_2019}.
The discharge data is available at a 0.1-degree resolution (approximately 10 km at the equator) and was extracted from the nearest flow-connected cell for each site; however, discharge data could not be obtained for two headwater stream sites, which were excluded from further analysis.
At each site, we log-transformed the discharge data and then scaled it by the temporal mean to facilitate comparison across sites with varying stream sizes.

We define ``flow variability'' as the magnitude of discharge deviations from seasonal expectations.
To model this variability, we fitted a Generalized Additive Model (GAM) as follows:

\begin{align}
    \begin{split}
    D_i &= \alpha_d + s(\mbox{Julian}_i) + \varepsilon_i,\\
    \varepsilon_i &\sim t(0, \sigma_d^2, \nu_d),
    \end{split}
\end{align}

where $D_i$ represents the scaled daily discharge, ``$\mbox{Julian}_i$'' refers to the Julian date (with the first day of the year as the origin), and $s(\cdot)$ denotes the smoothing function.
We used a Student's t-distribution for the error structure, as it better captures the fat-tailed nature of the discharge data.
The degree of freedom, $\nu_d$, characterizes the thickness of the tails, with smaller values indicating a higher likelihood of extreme high or low flows relative to seasonal expectations (i.e., fatter tails).
As $\nu_d$ approaches infinity, the Student's t-distribution converges to a normal distribution.

We regressed the log-transformed degrees of freedom ($\ln \nu_d$) on the log-transformed regional precipitation (i.e., watershed average; see Methods) using a linear mixed-effects model with random effects for watershed ID and hydrological region (see Figure 4).
The model revealed a significant negative scaling relationship, described by the equation $\ln \nu_d = 15.58 - 1.84 \, \ln \text{Precipitation} + \text{error}$ (SE for intercept: 3.99, SE for slope: 0.57).
This result suggests that watersheds with higher precipitation are more likely to experience extreme high or low flows relative to seasonal norms.

\subsection{Distance Ratio}

The distance ratio, $d_{w}$, was used to evaluate the randomness of the spatial sampling design within watershed $w$.
The procedure consists of the following steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Dummy sites were randomly generated along the river network. The number of dummy sites matched the number of actual sampling sites in the observations.
    \item Euclidean distance for each pair of sites was calculated, and the median distance was determined.
    \item Steps 1 and 2 were repeated 100 times to compute the average of the median distances, denoted as $\mu_d$.
    \item Finally, $d_{w}$ was calculated as the ratio of the observed median distance to $\mu_d$.
\end{enumerate}

Therefore, the distance ratio $d_{w}$ represents the spatial randomness of actual sampling sites, with values smaller (greater) than one indicating over-aggregation (over-dispersion) of sampling sites.
We assumed $d_{w} = 0$ if there was only one site within a watershed.

\subsection{Competing Model}

The hierarchical model presented in the main text is a random intercept model, which assumes no geographic variation in the effects of river length and branching rate. To allow for geographic variation, we developed a random intercept-slope model, where the effects of river length and branching rate vary by geographic region $u$.

Specifically, we modified the watershed-level regression equation as follows:

\begin{equation}
    \alpha_{0, w} = \beta_{0, u[w]} + \beta_{1, u[w]} \ln L_w + \beta_{2, u[w]} \ln \lambda_{b, w} + \sum_{m=3} \beta_m x'_{m, w} + \varepsilon_{w},
    \label{eq:watershed-average}
\end{equation}

and

\begin{equation}
    \boldsymbol{\beta_u} \sim \mbox{MVN}(\boldsymbol{\mu_{\beta}}, \Omega),
\end{equation}

where $\boldsymbol{\beta_u} = \{\beta_{0, u}, \beta_{1, u}, \beta_{2, u}\}$. Here, $\boldsymbol{\mu_{\beta}}$ represents the mean vector, and $\Omega$ denotes the variance-covariance matrix.
We used weakly informative priors for the mean vector $\boldsymbol{\mu_{\beta}}$, the standard deviations $\sigma_{\beta, m}$ for $\boldsymbol{\beta_u}$ (= diagonal elements in $\Omega$), and the upper-triangular Cholesky factor $U_{\Omega}$ for $\Omega$:  $\boldsymbol{\mu_{\beta}} \sim \mbox{Normal}(0, 5^2)$, $\sigma_{\beta, m} \sim t^+(0, 1, 10)$ ($m \in \{0, 1, 2\}$), and $U_{\Omega} \sim \mbox{LkjCholesky}(2.0)$.
The Cholesky LKJ correlation prior implies $U_{\Omega}^T U_{\Omega} = R_{\Omega} \sim \mbox{Lkj}(2.0)$, where $R_{\Omega}$ corresponds to the correlation matrix from $\Omega$. 
Other priors were specified as described in the main text.

Four Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) chains were run until parameter estimates
converged.
The total number of MCMC iterations per chain was 60,000, in which MCMC samples were saved every 60 steps after the initial 30,000 burn-in period.
As a result, we used a total of $500 \times 4 = 2,000$ MCMC samples for the estimation of posterior distributions.
Convergence was assessed by examining whether the R-hat indicator of each parameter approached < 1.1.
